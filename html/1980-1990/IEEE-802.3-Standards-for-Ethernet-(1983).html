About

The IEEE 802.3 standard of 1983 is one of the early standards that defines specifications for Ethernet technology, one of the most widespread networking technologies globally. This standard establishes fundamental parameters for data transmission over local area networks (LANs) using Ethernet technology.

This standard was a significant starting point for the development of Ethernet networks and contributed to defining the foundations for subsequent standards that introduced improvements such as higher transmission speeds and different physical link technologies.

Historical background

Ethernet has its roots in the research laboratories of Xerox's Palo Alto Research Center (PARC). In 1973, Robert Metcalfe and his colleague David Boggs developed the first prototype of the Ethernet system. The name "Ethernet" is derived from the theory of the ether, a outdated physical concept that Metcalfe found fitting to describe data transmission through the air. In 1976, Metcalfe founded 3Com Corporation to further develop the technology. A significant momentum came in 1980, when the Ethernet project was submitted to the IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) for standardization. In 1983, the IEEE officially ratified the IEEE 802.3 standard, providing the foundational specifications for Ethernet networks. This standard established a transmission speed of 10 Mbps and used a bus topology with the CSMA/CD access method. This provided a solid foundation for the growth and development of Ethernet networks. Before, Ethernet had been primarily used in research and academic environments, but, after standardization, it began to spread into commercial and industrial settings, accelerating its widespread adoption. After this period, Ethernet standards underwent further developments and improvements, including the introduction of higher transmission speeds, new network topologies, and new physical link technologies. Ethernet became the dominant network technology for many applications and continued to evolve over the years.

Features

This standard Ethernet became a versatile and widely used networking technology in multiple contexts.

  • Transmission Speed: The 1983 standard sets a transmission speed of 10 megabits per second (10 Mbps). This speed marked a significant improvement over previous technologies and contributed to faster communication in local area networks.
  • Bus Topology: The defined network topology in this standard is primarily a bus. In a bus topology, all devices share the same transmission medium, known as the "bus." This arrangement simplifies network construction and allows all nodes to communicate directly with each other.
  • CSMA/CD Access Method: The access method used is CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection). Before transmitting, each station listens to detect any transmission signals, and if the channel is clear, it proceeds with the transmission. In the event of a collision (two stations transmitting simultaneously), the system detects the collision, and the involved stations retry transmission at random subsequent times.
  • Ethernet Frame: AThe standard defines the structure of the Ethernet frame, which includes fields such as destination address, source address, protocol type, and data. This frame structure has been fundamental for the proper transmission and interpretation of data on the network.
  • Physical Link Protocols: The 1983 standards included variants of physical link protocols like 10BASE5 and 10BASE2. For instance, 10BASE5 used thick coaxial cables, while 10BASE2 used thin coaxial cables. These protocols specified the type of cable and connectors used for the physical connection between network devices.
  • MAC Addressing: MAC (Media Access Control) addressing is a fundamental part of the standard. Each Network Interface Card (NIC) has a unique MAC address, used to uniquely identify each device on the network.
  • Modular Structure: The 1983 standard was designed in a modular fashion, allowing the implementation of different physical link technologies and promoting interoperability between devices from different manufacturers.

From here there was a success and widespread adoption of Ethernet networks. Over the years, Ethernet standards have been revised and updated to support higher transmission speeds and adapt to the evolving needs of modern networks.
