IBM Deep Blue

IBM Deep Blue is powerful machine that made history when it beat the world chess champion in a match in 1997.


Artificial Intelligence

This event was a significant breakthrough for the field of artificial intelligence and informatics in general. Ever since the emergence of first computers and the idea of artificial intelligence, it was of great interest to scientists to compare the human mind and machine in their “thinking” abilities. Chess was picked as a good way of testing the abilities of computers, as it consists of a collection of challenging problems, yet the rules are very simple.


More about the Project

What started in 1985 as a student project called ChipTest at Carnegie Mellon University became an IBM research project called Deep Blue. In 1996, Deep Blue played against the world chess champion in a match highly covered by media, but it lost. The IBMers didn’t give up. They knew their machine could explore up to 200 million possible chess positions per second. Eventually, Deep Blue has won in the 1997 rematch. It was a six-game match: two wins for IBM, one for the champion and three draws.


Significance

The Deep Blue is significant not only because of chess, but it gave insight into ways to design a computer to tackle complex problems. The deep blue architecture itself was used in financial modelling (marketplace trends and risk analysis), data mining (uncovering hidden relationships and patterns in large databases), and molecular dynamics (a valuable tool for helping to discover and develop new drugs).
