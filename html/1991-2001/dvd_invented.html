Birth of DVDs

1996 marked a significant breakthrough for inexpensive distribution of digital entertainment media, as DVDs, which quickly became a hit, were introduced to the market.


About DVD

DVD is a type of optical disc that is utilized to store data and serves as a platform for multimedia. Its primary use in the market is for the playback of recorded movies and TV shows, which is why it is referred to as a "digital video disc". However, it can also be employed on personal computers for storing extensive amounts of various data types, as it comes in read-only, recordable, erasable, and rewritable versions. This versatility is the reason why it is known as a "digital versatile disc."


Technology

The DVD is the next iteration of compact disc (CD) technology. Research on storing high-quality video on the same disc began soon after Sony Corporation and Philips Electronics NV released the first audio CDs in 1982. In 1994-1995, two competing formats emerged: Sony and Philips introduced the Multimedia CD (MMCD), while Toshiba Corporation and Time Warner Inc. led a group that developed the Super Density (SD) disc. However, by the end of 1995, both groups reached a consensus and agreed on a common format called DVD, which incorporated elements from both proposals. The first DVD players were then made available for purchase in Japan in 1996.


Comparison

Similar to a CD drive, a DVD drive employs a laser to interpret digitally encoded information on the disc in the shape of small pits arranged in a spiral pattern from the center to the outer edge. Blue-Ray and others also share this technology but all use light of different wavelengths, allowing them to achieve different reading/writing precisions and therefore different storage densities, improving with each generation.
