The screen that got the "touch"

The history of the touch screen is a long one, starting in 1946, with the "DIRECT LIGHT PEN", designed for sports telecasting which, when placed against a CRT (cathode ray tube) display, it would amplify and add to the original signal, used for temporarily drawing circles or arrows onto a broadcast.
But the major innovations came at a later time, in 1962 the first version of a touchscreen which operated independendly of the display was patented by AT&T, which was operated with a stylus.
In 1965, the first finger driven touchscreen was developed by Eric Johnson, who described it in detail in an article published in 1967, and it was given a patent in 1969.
In 1968 Frank Beck and Bent Stumpe developed a transparent touchscreen, which was put to use in 1973. However, in an article describing their work, they indicated it was capable of multi-touch, but was inhibited, as it was considered not useful at the time.

Capacitive vs. Resistive touchscreens

Touchscreens don't have only one way of function. They can have various ways of sensing touch, but we will talk about the two methods that were used in the time.

Capacitive touchscreens

A capacitive touchscreen consists of an insulator, such as glass, coated with a transparent conductor. As the human body is also a conductor, touching the surface of the screen results in a disctortion of the electrostatic field, measurable as a change in capacitance.
That means that they cannot be used with insulating materials, since the finger cannot be detected, and so no touch.
However, this can give thinner and more accurate touch screens, such as the current AMOLED screens, with weight and thickness reduced.

Resistive touchscreens

A resistive touchscreen is composed of several thin layers, the most important of which are two transparent electrically resistive layers facing each other with a thin gap between. One layer has conductive connections along its sides, the other along top and bottom, with one passing electricity and the other sensing it. When a fingertip or a stylus presses down, the two layers touch to become connected. By rapidly switching layers, the position can be detected on screen.
They are used in places where there are liquids and possible contaminants, such as hospitals and restaurants, for their high tollerance and their low cost, and they can be operated with gloves.
