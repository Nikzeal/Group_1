The Development

Unix was a development project of AT&T, which started in 1969 at Bell Labs by Ken Thompson, Dennis Ritchie, and others. In the late 1970s, it was licensed to outside parties, including University of California, Berkeley, which made BSD, and from it would come out MacOS, Microsoft (Xenix), Sun Microsystems (Solaris), and IBM (AIX).
"Unix license would cost a company $20,000 (educational institutions were charged $200), but a few firms felt the improved productivity achieved with Unix was worth the fee." - David Fiedler, Byte-Magazine Vol. 8, Num. 10, Oct. 1983, pg.132
In the early 1990s, AT&T sold its rights in Unix to Novell, which then sold it to The Open Group, that allowed the use of the mark to who complies with the Single UNIX Specification.

Why Unix?

Unix was made to be a useful platform for programmers developing software. The system grew as it spread in academic circles, and users added their software and shared them among colleagues.

"Utility programs were added by various people as they had problems to solve.
Brilliant researchers wrote their own software and added it to Unix." - Shelley Powers, Unix Power Tools, p.3, O'Reilly Media, 2003.

As Unix gained popularity, multi-tasking and multi-user capabilities were added to it, and they are characterized by various concepts.

The Unix Philosophy

The Unix philosophy, originated by Ken Thompson, is based on the experience of leading developers, bringing conceps of modularity and reusability. The Unix philosophy for programs has four points:

  1. Do one thing and do it well.
  2. The output can be another's input.
  3. Have a clean code.
  4. Make a hard task easy.

The future birth of Linux

Linux is an open-source Unix-like operating system that was born in the 1990s, which is nowadays a father of many distros: various distributions with different views, programmers, packages and versions.
