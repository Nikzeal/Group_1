About the 4004

The Intel 4004 is a 4-bit CPU released by Intel in 1971. Sold for US$60 (equivalent to $450 in 2023), it was the first commerically produced microprocessor, and the first for Intel.
The 4004 was the first large-scale integration, showing the superiority of the MOS silicon gate technology, which ensures that the gate is naturally and precisely aligned to the edges of the source and drain, and in exchange duplicated the number of transistors with five times the speed.

An open Intel 4004

The innovative 4004 chip design served as a model on how to use the SGT for complex logic and memory circuits, accelerating the adoption of the new technology, developed by Fairchild by Federico Faggin, which was later improved in Intel 4040 in 1974.

Development

In April 1969, Busicom, a Japanese company that produced calculators and ATMs, approached Intel with a new design for an electronic calculator to compete with Olivetti Programma 101, one of the first programmable calculators. The difference would be in using integrated circuits and solid-state shift registers instead of printed circuits and expensive magnetostriction wire in the 101.
In contrast to earlier designs, a general-purpose processor concept was made, to later use it in cash registers and automatic teller machines. Intel assigned Marcian Hoff to act as the liaison between the two companies, who brought concern about the seven integrated circuits and the interconnections which would make Busicom's price goals impossible to meet.
He noticed that the compexity was due to every instructions being implemented separately, and suggested to support subroutine calls, but Busicom brought some specific issues, as some routines would use large amounts of ROM if implemented as subroutines.
Stanley Mazor joined Intel from Fairchild and with Hoff came up with solutions, resulting in an architecture that reduced the 7-chip Busicom design to a 4-chip Intel proposal. In April 1970 Faggin joined, introducing the SGT technology and "buried contacts" that allowed the silicon connecting wired to be directly connected to components, eliminating one step from the processing, which further simplified the chip and made it possible to create.

Uses

The first commercial use was the Busicom calculator 141-PF, and was also used in the first microprocessor-controlled pinball game, Bally, in 1974.
