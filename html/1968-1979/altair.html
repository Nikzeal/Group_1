General & Characteristics:

Altair 8800 poster

The Altair 8800 was created by Micro Instrumentation and Telemetry Systems (MITS) and released in January 1975. MITS, which stands for Micro Instrumentation and Telemetry Systems, was founded by United States Air Force veterans Ed Roberts and Robert Mims. Ed Roberts eventually bought out the company entirely.

The Altair 8800 was one of the earliest personal computers, available as a kit that users had to assemble. It featured the Intel 8080 CPU with a clock speed of 2.0 MHz and 256 bytes of RAM (expandable to a maximum of 64K). The system had a front panel with switches for user input and front panel LEDs for output. The Altair used the Altair Bus, a flexible expansion system that allowed the addition of various cards for enhanced functionality.

Problem it resolved:

The Altair 8800 addressed the challenge of making personal computers accessible to enthusiasts. It provided a "build it yourself" kit, offering hobbyists the opportunity to assemble their own desktop computers. The Altair's release marked a significant step in making computing more attainable for individuals without the need for extensive technical expertise.

Repercussion:

The release of the Altair 8800 in 1975 was a groundbreaking moment, popularizing microcomputers and laying the foundation for the personal computer revolution. Its significance lies in being one of the earliest affordable personal computers available to the public.

The Altair 8800 inspired individuals like Bill Gates and Paul Allen, who saw the potential for software development for these new machines. Gates and Allen created Altair BASIC, marking the birth of Microsoft and the start of a computing era. The Altair Bus also became an industry standard, fostering compatibility among various computer systems.

Conclusion:

The Altair 8800, introduced in 1975 by MITS, played a pivotal role in democratizing computing. By offering a kit that enthusiasts could assemble, it brought personal computers into the hands of individuals who were eager to explore the possibilities of computing.

Its impact resonates through the subsequent founding of Microsoft and the establishment of industry standards. The Altair 8800 is not only a historical milestone in the evolution of personal computing but also a symbol of the DIY spirit that fueled the early days of the computer revolution.
